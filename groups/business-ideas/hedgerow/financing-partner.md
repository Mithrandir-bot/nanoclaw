# Hedgerow — Financing-Partner Capital Models: Regulatory & Viability Analysis

**Date:** 2026-06-02
**Scope:** Evaluate two ways to bring outside capital into Hedgerow, a "self-insured house" that charges small businesses a flat fee and pays out when a defined event occurs, and now wants third-party capital to provide liquidity and bear payout risk.

- **(A)** Charge a partner a recurring SaaS/membership fee to be a "financing partner" who funds a dedicated capital pool backing payouts and earns a return.
- **(B)** Hedgerow keeps a % of the volume a financing partner originates/backs.

---

## 0. Threshold question that breaks the tie: Is Hedgerow's own product insurance?

Everything downstream depends on this. A product that takes a **fee** and pays a **defined amount on the occurrence of a fortuitous event the payer is exposed to** is the textbook definition of **risk transfer = insurance**. Whether you call the fee a "membership," "subscription," or "SaaS," substance controls. State unauthorized-insurer statutes explicitly sweep in "the collection of premiums, **membership fees, assessments, or other compensation**" for risk-bearing promises (see NAIC model and Texas Insurance Code Ch. 101, Nevada NRS 685B, Washington RCW 48.15). Regulators have fined non-insurance brands for exactly this packaging (e.g., WA fined Airbnb $20,000 as an unauthorized insurer; Avis settled ~$34M for selling supplemental liability coverage without authority).

There are only a few legitimate ways to NOT be "insurance":
1. **Parametric structuring** — pays a fixed amount on an objective trigger, not on proven loss. This is *broader* coverage, not an escape: most states still treat genuine risk transfer as insurance, and NY (Ins. Law §1113) now expressly defines and regulates parametric weather products. "Parametric" reduces claims friction; it does **not** reliably exempt you from licensing.
2. **Service-contract / warranty carve-out** — ~38 states adopted the NAIC Service Contract Model Act declaring service contracts "not insurance." This only works for product-tied service obligations (repair/replace the thing you sold), not for paying cash on an external business event. Probably unavailable to Hedgerow.
3. **Discretionary mutual** — pay claims at the members' *discretion*, not as a legal obligation (the Nexus Mutual model). This is a real path but requires a true mutual/membership legal structure and is itself heavily scrutinized; "discretion" must be genuine.

**Bottom line on the threshold:** Hedgerow is very likely conducting (or is one enforcement letter away from) the **business of insurance**. If it is unlicensed, the whole stack is already exposed, and bolting outside risk capital on top *amplifies* that exposure. **Resolve Hedgerow's own licensing/structure before designing any capital model.** The capital question is downstream of, and gated by, this.

## 1. Regulatory reality of the outside-capital layer (the crux)

When an outside party puts up money that bears insurance payout risk in exchange for a return, you trigger **two independent regulatory regimes simultaneously**. You must clear **both**.

### (i) Is it reinsurance / insurance regulation? — Almost certainly yes.

Capital that "bears some of the payout risk" for the insurer *is* the insurer's capital or is reinsurance. Reinsurance is the transfer of insurance liability from the cedent to a party that "assumes all or part of one or more insurance policies." That assuming party must be either:
- a **licensed/authorized reinsurer**, or
- a **collateralized / special-purpose reinsurer** (post 100% collateral via trust, LOC, or funds-withheld), or
- **rated paper via a fronting carrier** with the risk passed to a reinsurer behind it.

You cannot have a random "financing partner" stand behind insurance payouts for profit without it being characterized as carrying on insurance/reinsurance business. Doing so unlicensed is **unauthorized insurance** — per-act fines (e.g., up to $10,000/act in NV), Class C felony exposure in some states, back premium taxes, and cease-and-desist.

### (ii) Is it a security (Howey)? — Also almost certainly yes.

Howey: (1) investment of money (2) in a common enterprise (3) with an expectation of profits (4) derived from the efforts of others. A financing partner who contributes capital to a pool, expecting a return generated by Hedgerow's underwriting, pricing, and claims management, hits **all four prongs**. The interest in the capital pool is an **investment contract = a security**. Selling/managing it implicates Securities Act registration (or a valid exemption) and potentially **investment-adviser** and **broker-dealer** rules. Several states also apply the broader **"risk capital" test**, which catches arrangements where investors put capital at risk to fund an enterprise even more readily than Howey. Note the closest precedent — fractional interests in life-insurance payouts (life settlements) — has been litigated as securities (the *Barry*-line cases).

### Does calling it a "SaaS fee" or "membership" change anything? — No.

This is the most important answer in the report. Both regimes apply **substance over form**. The SEC: "form [is] disregarded for substance and the emphasis [is] on economic reality"; "format or labels do not control." Insurance statutes expressly list "membership fees" as a covered form of compensation for risk-bearing. **Re-labeling the consideration as a SaaS subscription does not convert (a) a risk-bearing capital interest into a non-security, or (b) an insurance/reinsurance arrangement into software.** If anything, an artificial label is an aggravating factor in an enforcement action (evidence of intent to evade).

A subtlety on Model (A): the *flat SaaS fee itself* might genuinely be software/platform revenue **if** it is truly decoupled from risk and return — i.e., the partner pays $X/month for access/tooling and any risk capital they post is governed by a *separate, properly structured* reinsurance + securities-compliant vehicle. But you cannot use the SaaS label to *paper over* the risk-capital relationship. The fee and the risk-capital interest must be analyzed separately, and the risk-capital interest will still be a security and still be (re)insurance.

### Is taking retail / individual capital to fund insurance payouts lawful without heavy licensing? — No. Avoid entirely.

Pooling money from individuals to back insurance losses, for a return, is the worst-case overlap: unlicensed insurance **plus** an unregistered securities offering of a pooled investment vehicle **plus** likely unregistered investment-company/adviser issues. This is precisely the fact pattern regulators pursue. Do **not** crowdfund payout capital from non-accredited people under Reg CF / Reg A. Even with accredited investors it must be wrapped in a proper reinsurance vehicle (below).

## 2. Analogous models — how outside capital legitimately backs a book

| Model | How outside capital enters | What it requires | Relevance to Hedgerow |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lloyd's "Names" / syndicate capital** | Members (corporate + individual "Names") post Funds at Lloyd's, take proportional profit/loss; backed by Lloyd's "chain of security" (£92.5bn syndicate assets + £30.5bn FAL + £2.9bn Central Fund, YE2024) | A regulated marketplace, managing agents, minimum capital, the central mutual backstop | The *aspirational* end-state, not a startup move. Shows risk capital + a central guarantee fund + heavy oversight. |
| **Fronting carrier + MGA + reinsurer** | A licensed/rated "fronting" carrier issues the policies an MGA produces; risk is ceded to reinsurers / ILS. Hybrid fronts retain 5–30% net. | A fronting partner, an MGA agreement, reinsurance treaties; reinsurer posts collateral unless authorized | **The cleanest realistic path.** Hedgerow becomes an MGA; a rated front issues compliant paper; outside capital enters *as the reinsurer/ILS behind the front*. |
| **ILS / catastrophe bonds** | Investors buy notes from an SPV; principal is at risk to defined triggers; ~$61.3bn outstanding, $20bn+ 144A issuance in 2025 | SPV (often Bermuda), **Rule 144A** offering to **QIBs only**, offering circular, limited-recourse/collateral language | Template for *securities-compliant* risk capital — but institutional-only, expensive, for scale. |
| **Reinsurance sidecars / collateralized reinsurers** | Investors fund a fully-collateralized SPV that takes a quota share of the book via participating pref/surplus notes | Bermuda BMA collateralized-insurer or SPI class; segregated cells; PPM to *sophisticated* investors | Best fit for a single committed capital partner backing Hedgerow's book in a compliant wrapper. |
| **Nayms / OnRe (onchain)** | Regulated smart-contract marketplace connecting capital to insurance risk | **Bermuda-licensed** framework | Shows onchain capital is possible *only inside* a real regulatory perimeter. |
| **Nexus Mutual (DeFi)** | Members stake capital; cover paid on a **discretionary** basis — "not a contract of insurance" | Genuine discretionary mutual; members vote on claims; now building *regulated* cells (w/ Ensuro, BMA-licensed) to touch real risk | The discretionary-mutual escape hatch — real but narrow, and Nexus itself is moving *into* regulation to scale. |
| **Vouch / program insurtechs** | Operate as MGA/program on rated paper, cede to reinsurers | Carrier partner + reinsurance | Confirms the MGA-on-rated-paper route is the standard insurtech playbook. |
| **Prize-indemnity reinsurance** | Promoter pays premium; a licensed insurer/reinsurer carries the contingent prize risk | Licensed (re)insurer | Closest functional analogue to "flat fee, defined-event payout" — and note it is carried by *licensed* insurers. |

**Common thread:** Every legitimate structure routes outside capital either (a) through a **licensed/rated carrier with reinsurance behind it**, (b) into a **collateralized SPV/sidecar sold only to QIBs/sophisticated investors under a securities exemption**, or (c) into a **genuine discretionary mutual**. There is no compliant version where an arbitrary partner pays a "fee" to bear insurance risk for profit outside one of these wrappers.

## 3. Viability & best execution for a pre-revenue startup

**Realistic now:**
- **Become an MGA and rent rated paper from a (hybrid) fronting carrier.** This is the fastest legitimate route — it bypasses the multi-year path to becoming a licensed insurer and gives Hedgerow compliant paper to write on immediately. Hedgerow focuses on distribution, pricing, and claims; the carrier holds the regulated risk; reinsurance/ILS sits behind it.
- **Bring outside capital in *as the reinsurer behind the front*,** via either (i) a treaty with an existing licensed reinsurer, or (ii) a **single committed capital partner** funding a **collateralized reinsurance vehicle / sidecar** (e.g., Bermuda collateralized-insurer or segregated cell), sold under **Reg D 506(c) to accredited/QIB investors** with a proper PPM and limited-recourse/collateral terms. One sophisticated capital partner is far cleaner than many.

**Later / at scale:**
- A dedicated **144A cat-bond/ILS** program (institutional, costly — only once there's a sizable, modelable book).
- Owning a **captive or licensed carrier**; a Lloyd's-style syndicate is years away, if ever.

**Cleanest compliant path (recommended sequence):**
1. Get insurance-regulatory counsel to classify Hedgerow's product (insurance vs. discretionary cover vs. service contract) **first**.
2. Stand up as an **MGA on a fronting carrier's rated paper** (or, if the discretionary-mutual route is chosen, build a *genuine* mutual).
3. Place outside risk capital **behind the front** as **collateralized reinsurance**, offered only to **accredited investors / QIBs** under a securities exemption, in a segregated/limited-recourse SPV.

**What to AVOID:**
- Crowdfunding payout capital from **non-accredited / retail** individuals (Reg CF/Reg A) — triple jeopardy: unlicensed insurance + unregistered securities + investment-company issues.
- Any "financing partner" bearing insurance risk **without** a fronting/reinsurance wrapper.
- Using **SaaS/membership labels to disguise** a risk-capital or insurance relationship — substance-over-form makes this worse, not safer.
- General solicitation of a capital pool before the securities exemption and the (re)insurance wrapper are in place.

---

## 4. Verdict

### Model (A) — SaaS/membership fee to be a "financing partner" funding a payout pool

**DO-NOT-DO as conceived → VIABLE-WITH-STRUCTURE if rebuilt.**
As literally described — a partner pays a recurring fee to fund a pool that bears payouts and earns a return — it is simultaneously (1) **unauthorized insurance/reinsurance** and (2) an **unregistered securities offering** (the pool interest is an investment contract under Howey). The "SaaS fee" label changes **nothing**; it is an aggravating factor. It only becomes viable if you **decouple** the two pieces: a genuine software/platform fee for tooling (fine), **and separately** route the risk capital through a **collateralized reinsurance vehicle behind a fronting carrier, offered to accredited investors/QIBs under a Reg D / 144A exemption.** At that point the "membership" framing is cosmetic and irrelevant; the real structure is fronting + reinsurance + a securities-exempt SPV.

### Model (B) — Hedgerow keeps a % of volume the financing partner originates/backs

**VIABLE-WITH-STRUCTURE — and the better of the two.**
Taking a percentage of volume is exactly how an **MGA earns** (commission/profit-share on the business it produces on a carrier's paper). This aligns Hedgerow with the standard, legitimate insurtech model and keeps the regulated risk on the front carrier and its reinsurers. The economics (a cut of volume) are clean; the constraint is purely structural: the "financing partner backing the volume" **must be a licensed reinsurer or a properly wrapped collateralized vehicle (accredited/QIB only) sitting behind a fronting carrier** — not an unlicensed party bearing risk directly. Build it that way and (B) is a sound, executable revenue model.

**One-line summary:** Fix Hedgerow's own insurance classification first; then the only compliant way to admit outside risk capital is **fronting carrier + reinsurance (or a collateralized sidecar sold to accredited/QIB investors)** — labels like "SaaS" or "membership" are legally irrelevant. (B) maps to that cleanly; (A) only works if rebuilt into it.
